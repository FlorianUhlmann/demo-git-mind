# 1. Introduction

> **One-line summary:** The infrastructure above the AI model is missing — the GitHub analogy for knowledge.
> **Navigation:** [INDEX.md](../INDEX.md) | Next: [02_problems.md](02_problems.md)

---

The billions going into AI right now are almost entirely focused on making the model better. More parameters. Better reasoning. Faster inference. But nobody is solving the layer above the model. The model is the "only" the engine. What's missing is the infrastructure that lets the engine's output accumulate into meaningful knowhow and new insights.

Our access to intelligence has outpaced the structures we have to structure and collaborate around knowledge. We are still capturing the outputs of thought in containers designed before a single person could explore a complex frontier in forty-five minutes and produce something genuinely new. PDFs and docs were built for conclusions. They have limited room for collaborative reasoning.

**We struggled with it before.**

Before version control, before GitHub, code collaboration looked exactly like knowledge collaboration looks today. You wrote something. You emailed it. Someone made changes and emailed it back. You had no idea what they changed or why. The reasoning behind every decision lived in someone's head or in an email chain nobody could find anymore. Two people working on the same problem simultaneously had to manually reconcile their work — reading through everything, guessing at intent, hoping nothing important was lost. Fields formed around whoever produced the most visible artifact. Contribution meant consuming what already existed, not adding to what was actively being built.

With GitHub the infrastructure changed. Not the code. The layer around the code. Suddenly every decision had a history. Every change had a reason attached. You could see not just what existed now but every step that produced it. You could find the exact moment something changed and understand why. You could contribute precisely — not by rewriting everything but by proposing a specific change to a specific place with a specific reason visible to everyone.

And something else happened that nobody fully predicted. Fields stopped forming around artifacts and started forming around problems. Open source communities didn't emerge because someone published a paper. They emerged because the reasoning was visible and the contribution point was clear. You could see exactly where a project was, what it needed, and where your thinking had something to add. The infrastructure didn't just make collaboration easier. It changed who could contribute, when, and at what depth.

Knowledge collaboration is exactly where code was before version control existed. The thinking is happening. The problems are being worked on. The constraints are being refined — in parallel, invisibly, with no shared infrastructure to make the reasoning visible or the contribution point findable.

GitMind is an environment designed to close the loop between knowledge creation, reasoning, and synthesis.
